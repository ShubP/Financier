# Compound Interest and Time in the Market

## What compounding is

Compound interest is earning returns on both your original money and on
the returns it has already generated. Each period's growth becomes part
of the base for the next period, so wealth accelerates over time rather
than growing in a straight line.

## Why time matters most

Because growth builds on prior growth, the number of years invested is
the most powerful lever a beginner has — often more powerful than the
exact rate of return or the amount contributed. Starting earlier, even
with small amounts, can beat starting later with larger amounts.

## A simple example

Investing $200 per month at a 7% average annual return:

- After 10 years: roughly $34,000 (you contributed $24,000).
- After 20 years: roughly $104,000 (you contributed $48,000).
- After 30 years: roughly $244,000 (you contributed $72,000).

The contributions grew modestly, but the compounding did the heavy
lifting in the later years.

## The Rule of 72

To estimate how long money takes to double, divide 72 by the annual
return. At 8%, money doubles in about 9 years (72 / 8). At 4%, it takes
about 18 years. The rule is a quick mental shortcut, not an exact
formula.

## Contributions and consistency

Regular, automatic contributions harness compounding without requiring
you to time the market. Reinvesting dividends and interest keeps the
compounding engine running. Fees and taxes work in reverse — they
compound against you — which is why low-cost, tax-advantaged investing
matters so much over decades.

## Takeaways

- Start early and stay invested; time is your biggest advantage.
- Automate contributions and reinvest distributions.
- Minimize fees so more of the compounding works for you.
