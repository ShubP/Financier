# Stocks and Equities

## What a stock is

A stock (also called a share or equity) represents partial ownership in
a company. When you buy one share, you own a small slice of that
business and have a claim on a proportional part of its assets and
future profits. Companies issue stock to raise money to grow.

## How investors make money

There are two main ways a stock can reward an investor:

- **Capital appreciation:** the share price rises above what you paid,
  so the position is worth more if you sell.
- **Dividends:** some companies distribute part of their profits to
  shareholders, usually each quarter. Not all companies pay dividends;
  many growth companies reinvest profits instead.

Total return combines both price change and dividends received.

## Common vs. preferred shares

- **Common stock** usually carries voting rights and variable
  dividends. Common shareholders are last in line if a company is
  liquidated.
- **Preferred stock** typically pays a fixed dividend and has priority
  over common stock for dividends and assets, but usually no votes.

## Risks to understand

Stocks are volatile: prices move daily on company news, earnings,
interest rates, and overall market sentiment. Individual companies can
underperform or fail, so a single stock can lose much or all of its
value. Historically, a broad basket of stocks has produced strong
long-term returns, but with meaningful short-term ups and downs. The
longer your time horizon, the more short-term volatility you can
generally afford to ride out.

## Practical takeaways

- Owning many stocks (via an index fund) reduces the risk tied to any
  one company.
- Short-term prices are unpredictable; long-term ownership of
  productive businesses is where most equity returns come from.
- Only invest money in stocks that you will not need for several years.
