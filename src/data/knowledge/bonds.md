# Bonds and Fixed Income

## What a bond is

A bond is a loan you make to a borrower — typically a government or a
corporation. In exchange, the borrower agrees to pay you periodic
interest (the "coupon") and to return the original amount (the "face
value" or "principal") on a set maturity date. Because the cash flows
are defined in advance, bonds are called fixed income.

## Key terms

- **Coupon rate:** the annual interest the bond pays, as a percent of
  face value.
- **Maturity:** when the principal is repaid (short, intermediate, or
  long term).
- **Yield:** the return you actually earn given the price you paid;
  yields move opposite to bond prices.
- **Duration:** a measure of how sensitive a bond's price is to
  interest-rate changes. Longer duration means bigger price swings.

## Why prices move

When prevailing interest rates rise, existing bonds that pay lower
coupons become less attractive, so their prices fall. When rates fall,
existing bonds rise in value. This inverse relationship is central to
understanding fixed income.

## Types of bonds

- **Government bonds** (e.g., U.S. Treasuries) carry very low default
  risk and are considered among the safest assets.
- **Municipal bonds** are issued by states and cities, often with tax
  advantages.
- **Corporate bonds** pay higher yields to compensate for higher
  default risk; ratings agencies grade them from investment grade down
  to high-yield ("junk").

## Role in a portfolio

Bonds usually move more calmly than stocks and can rise when stocks
fall, so they cushion a portfolio during downturns and provide steady
income. A common pattern is to hold more bonds as you approach a goal
or retirement, trading some growth for stability.
