# Dollar-Cost Averaging and Investor Behavior

## Dollar-cost averaging (DCA)

Dollar-cost averaging means investing a fixed amount on a regular
schedule — say $300 every month — regardless of whether prices are up
or down. When prices are low, your fixed amount buys more shares; when
prices are high, it buys fewer. Over time this can lower your average
cost per share and removes the pressure to guess the perfect moment.

## Why it helps beginners

DCA turns investing into a habit and takes emotion out of the decision.
You are never trying to call the top or bottom; you simply keep buying.
Automating contributions (for example, from each paycheck) makes the
behavior effortless and consistent.

## The trouble with market timing

Trying to jump in and out of the market to dodge downturns sounds
appealing but is extremely hard to do well. Missing just a handful of
the market's best days — which often cluster right after the worst days
— can sharply reduce long-term returns. "Time in the market beats
timing the market" captures the lesson: staying invested usually beats
trying to be clever about entry and exit.

## Behavioral pitfalls to avoid

- **Panic-selling** during downturns locks in losses and misses the
  recovery.
- **Performance chasing** — buying whatever just went up — often means
  buying high.
- **Overtrading** raises costs and taxes and rarely improves returns.

## A calmer approach

- Decide an allocation you can hold through a downturn.
- Automate regular contributions and ignore the daily noise.
- Rebalance on a schedule rather than reacting to headlines.

Discipline and patience, not prediction, are what most successful
long-term investors rely on.
