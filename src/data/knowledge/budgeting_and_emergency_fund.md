# Budgeting and the Emergency Fund

## Budgeting basics

A budget is simply a plan for where your money goes. The goal is not
restriction for its own sake but making sure spending matches your
priorities and leaves room to save and invest.

## The 50/30/20 guideline

A popular starting framework splits after-tax income into:

- **50% needs:** housing, food, utilities, transport, minimum debt
  payments.
- **30% wants:** dining out, entertainment, hobbies, travel.
- **20% savings and debt paydown:** emergency fund, retirement,
  extra payments on high-interest debt.

The exact percentages are a starting point, not a rule; adjust them to
your situation and cost of living.

## The emergency fund

An emergency fund is cash set aside for unexpected events — a job loss,
medical bill, or major repair. It keeps a surprise from forcing you
into high-interest debt or selling investments at a bad time.

- **How much:** a common target is three to six months of essential
  expenses. People with variable income may want more.
- **Where:** keep it safe and liquid — a high-yield savings account is
  typical, not the stock market, because you may need it on short
  notice and cannot risk a downturn.

## High-interest debt comes first

Paying off high-interest debt (such as credit cards) is often the best
"investment" you can make, because avoiding, say, 20% interest is a
guaranteed return that few investments can match. Build a small starter
emergency fund, then attack high-interest debt aggressively.

## Putting it together

1. Track income and spending for a month to see reality.
2. Build a small starter emergency fund (e.g., $1,000).
3. Pay down high-interest debt.
4. Grow the emergency fund to 3–6 months of expenses.
5. Invest consistently for long-term goals.
