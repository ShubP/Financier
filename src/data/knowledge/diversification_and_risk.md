# Diversification, Asset Allocation, and Risk

## Diversification

Diversification means spreading money across many investments so that
no single one can sink your portfolio. Different assets respond
differently to events, so a loss in one can be offset by stability or
gains in another. The practical lesson: don't put everything in one
stock, sector, or asset class.

## Asset allocation

Asset allocation is how you split a portfolio among major asset classes
— typically stocks, bonds, and cash. It is the single biggest driver of
both your long-term return and how bumpy the ride feels. A classic
illustrative split is "100 minus your age" in stocks, with the rest in
bonds, but the right mix depends on your goals, time horizon, and
comfort with volatility.

## Risk tolerance vs. risk capacity

- **Risk tolerance** is emotional: how much volatility you can stomach
  without panic-selling.
- **Risk capacity** is financial: how much loss your timeline and
  finances can actually absorb.

A long horizon raises capacity (you have time to recover); money needed
soon should sit in safer assets regardless of tolerance.

## Measuring risk

**Volatility** (often standard deviation) captures how much returns
swing around their average. Higher volatility means a wider range of
outcomes. Stocks are more volatile than bonds; bonds are more volatile
than cash. A **diversification score** rewards spreading weight across
many holdings rather than concentrating in one.

## Rebalancing

Over time, winners grow to dominate a portfolio, quietly raising its
risk. **Rebalancing** — periodically trimming what has grown and topping
up what has lagged back to your target mix — keeps risk in check and
enforces a disciplined "buy low, sell high."

## Takeaways

- Decide your target allocation first, then pick funds to fill it.
- Concentration is the main avoidable risk; diversify broadly.
- Rebalance on a schedule, not on emotion.
