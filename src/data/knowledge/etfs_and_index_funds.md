# ETFs, Mutual Funds, and Index Investing

## Funds bundle many securities

A fund pools money from many investors to buy a basket of securities.
Buying one fund share gives you a slice of everything it holds, which
is an easy way to diversify with a single purchase.

## ETFs vs. mutual funds

- **ETFs (exchange-traded funds)** trade on an exchange throughout the
  day like a stock. They are usually low cost and tax efficient.
- **Mutual funds** are bought and sold once per day at the closing net
  asset value. Some are actively managed and charge higher fees.

Both can hold stocks, bonds, or a mix.

## Index funds and passive investing

An **index fund** aims to match a market index (such as the S&P 500)
rather than beat it. Because it simply holds the index constituents, it
charges very low fees and tends to outperform most actively managed
funds over long periods — largely because high fees and trading costs
are a persistent drag on active managers.

## Expense ratios matter

The **expense ratio** is the annual fee a fund charges, expressed as a
percent of assets. A 0.03% index fund costs $3 per year per $10,000
invested; a 1.0% active fund costs $100. Over decades, that difference
compounds into a large gap in ending wealth. Favor low expense ratios.

## Why beginners often start here

Broad, low-cost index ETFs (total U.S. market, total international,
total bond) let a beginner own thousands of companies and bonds
instantly, with minimal cost and no need to pick individual winners.
This is the foundation of many simple, durable portfolios.

## Quick checklist

- Prefer low expense ratios (well under 0.20% for broad index funds).
- Understand what the fund holds before buying.
- Diversify across asset classes and geographies with a few funds.
